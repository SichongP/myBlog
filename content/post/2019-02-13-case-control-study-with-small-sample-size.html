Background

A breed-specific disease was discovered in horses and we would like to identify its causal variant. Let’s suppose we have whole genome sequences of 3 affected horses and 4 unaffected horses. A common startegy would be a case-control study identifying variants significantly associated with affected cohort. However, a limitation behind this approach is our sample size: with 3 cases and 4 controls, the best association we can get is:

fisher.test(rbind(c(6,0), c(0,8)))$p.value #Diploidy
## [1] 0.0003330003

This can never pass a Bonferroni-corrected \(\alpha\) which is usually at 1e-09 level! This is quite often a problem for researchers when samples are very hard to obtain. And it gets worse if we’re dealing with anything other than recessive traits! A less stringent \(\alpha\) (say 0.001)is certain to give us tons of false positives: for a dataset with 2 million variants, an average of \(0.001*2*10^6=2000\) false positives!

One way to get around of this problem is by utilizing the fact that this disease is breed-specific. This means that we are looking at a mutation that is fairly new: no earlier than the breed’s founding population!
